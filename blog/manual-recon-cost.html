The hidden cost of manual reconciliation — and how to calculate yours

revzio Team · May 2026 · 6 min read

Manual reconciliation has an obvious cost: hours. But the real cost is usually hidden — the downstream effects of late exceptions, inconsistent review, and audit scramble that taxes the entire org. Finance teams feel it as weekend overtime and “close fatigue.” Leaders feel it as delayed reporting and audit risk.

The visible cost: time spent in the close

Start with a simple calculator. It won’t capture everything, but it will make the baseline undeniable.

Close labor cost estimate

Use this formula:

(team size) × (close days) × (hours/day) × (loaded hourly rate)

Example: 6 people × 8 days × 7 hours/day × $85/hr ≈ $28,560 per close cycle.

The hidden cost: errors and restatements

When reconciliation is manual, exceptions are discovered late. Late exceptions lead to rushed judgment calls and inconsistent documentation. The cost of a material error isn’t just a correction — it’s credibility loss, management distraction, and potential audit findings. For mid-market companies, the business impact can be weeks of remediation and rework across finance and IT.

The audit multiplier

Audit costs rise when workpapers are assembled manually. If an auditor asks, “Why does this balance move?” and the answer is spread across spreadsheets, email threads, and screenshots, you pay twice: your team’s time plus auditor time. Many firms bill in the $300–$500/hr range for senior work. If reconciliation documentation is weak, the audit expands.

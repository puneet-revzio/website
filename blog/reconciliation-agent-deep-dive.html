How Orion's Reconciliation Agent closed 95% of items without human touch

revzio Team·May 2026·8 min read

Most reconciliation tools are built for totals. They tell you whether two balances tie out, not whether each line item is correct. Finance teams feel this gap every month: a bank balance matches, but the details don’t; a subledger agrees, but there are stale items hiding in the noise; a payout total ties, but the underlying remittance mapping is a maze.

Orion’s Reconciliation Agent was designed for the real problem: line-level matching across multiple sources that arrive at different times, with different identifiers, and with different representations of the same event. When it closes 95% of items without human touch, it’s not “autopilot” in the reckless sense — it’s governed automation that prepares work continuously and routes only true exceptions to reviewers.

The matching problem: why totals aren’t enough

Totals-based recon is fragile. If a single transaction is duplicated, misclassified, or delayed by a day, totals can still tie while the underlying ledger is wrong. Line-level recon is harder because identifiers don’t line up: the bank references a batch, the ERP references an invoice, and the processor references a payout. Human recon teams bridge those gaps with context and pattern recognition — but doing that manually is slow, inconsistent, and difficult to audit.

How Orion’s matching engine works

Orion reconciles continuously. Instead of waiting for month-end exports, it ingests events as they land (bank, billing, ERP, subledger) and normalizes them into a common model. Then it applies a matching engine that supports one-to-one, one-to-many, and many-to-many relationships.

Where rules are deterministic (exact invoice number, exact amount, exact date window), Orion uses them. Where reality gets messy (split payments, partial refunds, timing differences, fee netting), Orion learns patterns from your business logic and applies them consistently. The key is not “AI magic” — it’s repeatable matching behavior with evidence attached.

What “plain-English exceptions” actually means

Exceptions aren’t just “unmatched.” Orion flags an exception with a reason and next best action: “Amount differs by $0.37 due to processing fee,” “Possible duplicate: same vendor, same amount, same day,” or “Likely timing difference: expected settlement in 2 business days.” That context cuts review time dramatically because reviewers spend their time deciding, not hunting.

The 95% stat, with context

Teams that hit 95% aren’t ignoring edge cases — they’re surfacing them earlier. Continuous matching reduces the pile-up effect where issues are discovered late and fixed under deadline pressure. With Orion running daily (or more often), the remaining 5% becomes a steady stream of high-signal items: true breaks, policy questions, and anomalies worth attention.

What your team does with the remaining 5%

Finance still owns judgment. Orion routes exceptions into reviewer queues with evidence, suggested matches, and policy gates. Your team approves overrides, validates unusual cases, and signs off. Every decision is captured in the audit trail — so close week is confirmation, not reconstruction.
